1. Transient Matching

1.1. Motivation

  • Canonical market design setting: agents
    • know everything and
    • are matched once and for all
  • But in practice, e.g., on the econ job market…
    • I don't know my fit at Princeton;
    • I don't know Andrew's type; and
    • We can move between institutions after a few years
  • How should we think about learning and rematching?
    • What does equilibrium look like?

1.2. Contribution

Tractable bandit model of transient matching in decentralized markets

  1. Every period, each worker applies to one firm
  2. Firms accept workers above quality cutoff (endogenous)
  3. If successful, workers learn fit at firm. Else only learn rejection.
  4. Repeat Steps 1-3

Examine steady state of this system (unique if prefs aligned)

1.3. Results and policy implications

  • Rationalizes stylized facts in data
    1. Higher-quality workers search less
    2. Worker tenure increases with age
  • Equilibria generally inefficient: competition depresses incentive to search

Confirms some common policies are effective, but not others.

  • Headhunters are better for low-quality workers; raise efficiency
  • Unemployment benefits depresses search; lower efficiency

1.4. I like the paper!

  • Ambitious!
  • Theoretically sophisticated yet tractable
    • No clean characterization of optimal dynamic matching protocol
    • This paper takes one step in that direction
  • Has useful policy implications

1.5. Possible concerns + extensions

  • In bandit model, independence between arms is key
    • Interpretation: my chance of getting hired at MIT is independent of my chance of getting hired at Harvard, conditional on class
    • Can we relax independence?
      • Hard but see recent work in CS/OM.
  • Transitional dynamics?
    • Sudden influx of Theory/IO candidates in one year?
  • Take this model to data?

2. BTO: Endogenous Supply in Centralized Mechanisms

2.1. Summary

  • How should the designer of a waitlist supply goods, knowing earlier realizations of demand?
    • Relevant to food banks, class schedules, public housing…
  • Context: Public housing allocation in Singapore
  • Model: 2 queues (studio vs. large apartment), 2 types of agents with horizontal preferences
    • Planner minimizes \( \alpha \) misallocation + \( (1-\alpha) \) unassignment.
    • Each period: New agent born, unknown type. Simultaneously,
      • Agent chooses which queue to join;
      • Planner chooses which type of house to supply.

2.2. Results

  • Naive mechanism: Build studio iff previous person joins queue for studios.
  • BUT doesn't work if few people prefer studios AND are impatient!
    • No studios built? "Suck it up" and queue for large apartments.
  • Theorem 1: Optimal mechanism oversupplies underdemanded housing.
  • Theorem 2: Competition improves match quality.
    • Opposite when supply is exogenous!
  • Policy Recommendation: Batch applications to artificially generate competition.

2.3. What (I think) the paper does well

  • Tractable model of a waitlist with endogenous supply
  • Applicable to many settings with horizontal preferences
  • Can assess degree of mismatch in data (see paper)

2.4. Extensions

  • Some of the welfare loss from mismatch is from aggregate uncertainty
    • Because batching helps improve efficiency!
  • What happens when the mix of types changes over time?
    • How quickly can the planner detect these changes?
  • Empirical application with richer preference heterogeneity?
    • See Lee, Ferdowsian, and Yap (2023)
